Introduction

In the current e-book landscape, major players like Amazon, Barnes & Noble, and Apple have consolidated the traditional functions of selling, printing, and distribution. Especially when associated with current platform lock-in (due mostly to DRM), this suppresses both competition and cooperation (together with their associated creativity). We want to create an open environment and marketplace characterized by a diversity of creators, suppliers, and vendors of all sorts.

sBooks reinvents the experience of reading and the business of publishing to encourage creation, conversation, and engagement with interesting ideas. Its success will enable further transformations in education, research, public policy, and our collective intelligence.

sBooks is an open e-publishing and e-reading platform. Our target markets include:

  • readers: including people who buy books as well as libraries and their patrons:
  • authors: people who create books and other content;
  • publishers: people and organizations who support authorship and help to create the fit of author to market;
  • booksellers: people and organizations who enable and encourage discovery around content and meta-content;
  • meta-publishers: people and organizations who create overlays (free and paid) on existing content (free and paid)

This list is so long because we're creating a new publishing ecosystem with many different roles which need to be populated. The good news is, that for the most part, the incremental change required by any individual or organization is relatively small.

Our "product" looks different for each of these classes of customers. Here's a breakdown.

FOR READERS

sBooks provide two key advantages to readers: reading and rights.

In the area of reading, sBooks provide a better reading experience through:

  • improved interface: sBooks are easier to navigate and browse;
  • social reading: sBooks can be read by groups of individuals, supporting, encouraging and challenging one another;
  • overlays: notes, links, and tags created by individuals or organizations for existing books; and
  • smart search: books become naturally non-linear when there is effective indexing and search.

In the area of rights, sBooks distinguishes itself in two ways:

  • sBooks is an open web-based platform enabling anyone to publish sBook content through their own sites. Our primary delivery platform is late-model web browsers, whether on tablets, desktops, phones, or whatever comes next. There is no device-lock-in.
  • when we sell sBooks ourselves, we do so under a license which attempts to grant the rights which people have come to accept for physical books.

In developing our products for readers, we have two goals:

  • creating an outstanding user experience;
  • supporting books as long-term personal artifacts.

We get revenues from readers in at least four ways:

  1. when they buy an sBook (from anyone), they may need to pay to use our service for sharing or storing annotations;
  2. when they join a closed reading group or purchase a paid overlay, we get a piece of that transaction;
  3. when they buy an sBook through us, we provide (1) for free but take a piece of the purchase;
  4. we provide a subscription service which allows readers to upload their own books for use with sBooks. We correlate what they upload with our own books and other uploads, allowing the sBooks annotation and sharing features to work with uploaded books.

FOR AUTHORS

We want to encourage authors to create content for sBooks. Our chief advantages for authors are:

  1. high royalty rates: they get more per title, with minimal overhead;
  2. after-market revenue: when authors publish with us, they get a slice of our profit from reading groups and overlays;
  3. responsive payment: we use digital payment technologies to deliver royalty payments almost instantaneously;
  4. access to their audience through a variety of means, including social networks and dedicated overlays;
  5. pricing flexibility: authors can control maximum, minimum, and discount prices (see below);
  6. authoring tools: we provide tools for creating, editing, and producing sBooks; we also (incidentally) provide tools for generating e-books in other formats (ePub and Kindle) from their publications.

We may charge separately for certain parts of 4+6. Otherwise, we get revenue from sales, subject to a pricing model. The pricing model explicitly includes booksellers as a means for book discovery and marketing.

The pricing model has two basic parameters:

  • the "sale price" what the reader pays for the book;
  • the "discount price" is the amount guaranteed for each sale; the author/publisher gets at least 80% of this (minus payment service transaction costs);

The author or publisher can also set minimums and maximums for the sale price and receives 10% of the markup between the discount price and the sale price. If the sale price is below the discount price, the seller pays the difference to the publisher (with a slice to us).

FOR PUBLISHERS

All of the advantages for authors also apply to publishers. We believe that publishers have a crucial role in the book ecosystem. Part of this role is to help authors with creating their books, including editorial support, rights clearance, and custom content (graphics, images, video, etc).

Another important role for publishers is managing the fit of authors and markets to enhance both impact and revenue. In the digital age, an individual author and easily write something and distribute it globally; the challenge is getting it written and distributed so that it is read! Some rare authors have a gift for this, but most do not. That is the challenge where the publisher's role is most important, not just marketing, but advising and adjusting the content and design of a work to make a publication with greater impact and reach.

Publishing, especially in the English-speaking world, is still dominated by the "big six" publishing houses: . These houses, which have mostly grown by mergers and acquisitions, contain dozens of branded imprints and exist alongside hosts of smaller publishers. But in our digital age, the advantages of scale in publishing are reduced. We expect that this will lead to the development of "micro-publishing" where individuals with a vision and associated skills will help authors manage their fit to markets while also coordinating support from editors, designers, legal experts, and other creatives.

"Micropublishing" is about high quality lean production and is close to what Guy Kawasaki has recently started calling artistanal publishing.

While we will do what we can to make "Big Six" titles available as sBooks, our primary market consists of smaller publishers or nascent "micropublishers" with a vision which can become a brand.

Our initial offering to micropublishers will be our platform for publishing and, on the flip side, our platform for supporting writing and editing. We will probably offer a free version of the platform to micropublishers with a premium tier providing more functionality and support.

In the medium term, we will create a services marketplace for micropublishers, providing access to editors, artists, coaches, and even legal experts. There are various companies which already provide this kind of marketplace and we might partner with one (or several) of these companies.

In the medium to longer term, we hope to support micropublishers financing needs by providing a platform for "crowdfunding" particular titles. This could provide financing for book creation and preparation while also providing a built-in team of marketers and evangelists when a book is released.

A further advantage of sBooks to publishers (and authors) lies in the potential to check for proof of purchase on priced e-books before allowing some (or any) sBooks features to be used with a book. For example, every book sale could have a crypotgraphically validated code associating a particular user (identified by, for example, an email address) with a particular title from a particular publisher. Our sBooks service could then provide only limited functionality for a particular title without the presence of a valid proof of purchase.

FOR BOOKSELLERS

In the current e-book marketplace, the technology providers sell books directly to readers, leaving booksellers with little or no role.

In the traditional bound book marketplace, nearly all sales went through booksellers, who were also the principal means by which readers discovered new books. Booksellers also provided "tactical marketing" for their physical neighborhoods and simplified marketing for publishers: their intermediate customers were bookstores rather than readers.

Both of these roles can still be important in the e-book marketplace while becoming mostly independent of physical location. Currently, readers, publishers, and technology providers are still trying to sort out e-book discovery and creating incentives for online booksellers --- who will target particular audiences --- is one promising channel. In addition, booksellers can create community around the books they sell, a key to the kind of engagement which will drive after-market revenue.

For these reasons, booksellers are directly included in the sBooks model and pricing system. Booksellers make money not through direct sales, but through referrals which operate like an associates program. The bookseller gets 80% of the difference between the sale price and the discount price.

Those referrals allow the creation of various kinds of bundles, including bundles integrating overlays of various sorts. Some overlays may only be available through particular booksellers, creating opportunities for sellers to create distinguished products and to leverage their expertise at understanding and shaping the communities they serve.

FOR META-PUBLISHERS

These are the most hypothetical of our customers, consising of individuals and organizations who create overlays on sBooks. These overlays can provide explanation, critique, or context. Using overlay tags, they can make books more searchable and browsable, especially for particular audiences or viewpoints. Using overlay links, they can use images, sound, or even applications to enrich a book's core content.

There are three existing kinds of content creators who we expect will naturally become meta-publishers: educators, bloggers, booksellers, and the publishers themselves.

EDUCATORS already create content around existing books and provide that content, usually delivered personally, to the individuals and communities they teach. By creating and distributing this content as overlays to sBooks, this will improve the reach of great educators while supporting their work. It will also allow on-site teachers to leverage great resources while personalizing the learning experience for students skills and interests.

BLOGGERS already create new content and discover existing content, mostly from the ocean of the World Wide Web. Bloggers provide curation, opinion, explanation, and argument, all of which are just as important in the context of a complex text as for the web. They are also technologically savvy and generally have good market instincts. Meta-publishing will provide another channel for engagement and revenue.

BOOKSELLERS, as reimagined above, create value through discover and aggregation, including the aggregation of content with metapublished overlays. These overlays can provide the usual context or critique and can also provide links between bundled books. Because it will be possible to discount or limit overlays to versions of a book purchased from a particular seller, this creates an incentive for sellers to create valuable overlays.

PUBLISHERS who provide core content may also metapublish, either for their own content or other free or third-party content. Metapublishing will allow publishers to increase the revenue from book sales by unbundling (and charging additionally for) enriched content and context. Production costs for overlays should be significantly lower than for core content, allowing the creation of premium content with particularly high margins.

Core Services

sBooks is based around three core services:

  • an annotation service allowing readers to create, share, and apply glosses (notes, tags, links, and additional rich content) to e-books;
  • an overlay marketplace supporting the aggregation and metapublishing of collections of glosses from a particular source (for example, an expert) or community (for example, a class or other association);
  • a point of sale for e-books designed to work with the annotation service and overlay marketplace;
  • a publishing environment to help in the production of e-books for availability through the point of sale and other channels.

The current sBooks Terms of Service, drafted with Mintz-Levin, mostly covers the use and operation of the annotation service. It may need to be extended to include the overlay marketplace and certainly to include ebooks which are sold.

I am assuming that we need to have some kind of legal language --- contract, license, etc --- around anywhere that money changes hands, particularly in the overlay marketplace and at the point of sale. I'd like to use the various online adaptive payment schemes to have transactions immediately credit to authors, publishers, and metapublishers, hoping that this will simplify the legal aspects. But I may be naive.

I'm not shy about wanting to have legal language which gives readers more rights than they currently get for e-books.
