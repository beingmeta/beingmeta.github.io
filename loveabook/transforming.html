Transforming

precious volumes for a digital future

For many precious books, electronic files are not available, meaning that the content of the book has to be recovered by optical scanning and OCR. Many organizations have been scanning books over the past decade, including the Internet Archive and Google. Unfortunately, just scanning a book doesn't translate its enchantment to the digital world. In addition to inevitable OCR errors, scanned books are filled with artifacts from the printed page which make them difficult for print-disabled readers to easily access.

In addition, nearly any bound paper book was carefully (often lovingly) designed to fit its content to its particular form. This might range from the flow of text across pages, the selection and placement of pictures or tables, the sizing and style of typefaces, and the inclusion of notes or asides alongside the flow of the text. Sadly, little or none of this design makes it into the electronic version. Practically, books need to be redesigned as e-books, taking into account their structure and navigation, as well as the need to support pages whose dimensions or type sizes may be changed by the reader.

Finally, modern e-books provide many ways to enrich content with links, explanation, rich media, or social features. There is an opportunity to enhance books' enchantment and accessibility in their new medium.

Practically, the transformation of bound books into high quality e-books inolves five stages of work:

  1. Scanning and OCR (largely done by other projects)
  2. Bibliographical registration (partially done, to determine context and copyright)
  3. Proofing, correction, and structural tagging, correcting the inevitable errors introduced by computers and identifying relationships (footnotes, asides, etc) encoded in the original design.
  4. Redesign of the book for electronic reading, including the introduction of structure, styling of sections, and layout constraints
  5. Enrichment of the book by the tasteful and optional addition of images, videos, sounds, explanatory links, and rich indexes.
